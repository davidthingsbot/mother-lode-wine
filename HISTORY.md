# History of Mother Lode Wine Country

## The Gold Rush Era (1849–1870s)

Wine grapes arrived in the Sierra Foothills with the California Gold Rush. As fortune seekers — many of them European immigrants with winemaking knowledge — streamed into the Mother Lode, they brought viticulture with them. When placer mining began to diminish, many prospectors turned to a more settled way of life, planting orchards and vineyards.

In 1855, the California State Legislature passed a law exempting newly planted grape vines from taxation for four years, spurring rapid growth:

- **El Dorado County:** 24,000 vines in 1856 → 77,500 in 1858
- **Tuolumne County:** 9,000 vines in 1857 → 50,000 in 1858

The first foothills winery was established in 1856 near Plymouth in the Shenandoah Valley — making it the fourth oldest winery in California.

## The Golden Age (1870s–1890s)

By the 1870s and 1880s, the foothill counties ranked among California's major wine producers. An 1861 San Francisco Bulletin front-page story titled "Vineyards in the Foothills" brought regional attention.

As Leon Adams wrote in *The Wines of America*:

> "By 1890, more than 100 wineries were operating at such locations as Nevada City, Colfax, Lincoln, Penryn, Auburn, Placerville, Coloma, Shingle Springs, Ione, Volcano, Jackson, San Andreas, Sonora, Columbia, and Jamestown."

At its peak, El Dorado County alone had approximately 8,000 of the region's 10,000 total vineyard acres.

## The Decline (1890s–1930s)

Several factors contributed to the collapse of foothill viticulture:

1. **Economic decline** — The end of gold mining caused population exodus
2. **Phylloxera** — The devastating vine disease struck the region
3. **Prohibition (1920–1933)** — The final blow to commercial winemaking

By 1930, vineyards were largely replaced by orchards of peaches and prunes. Wine grape cultivation shifted to the valley lowlands.

## The Revival (1970s–Present)

After decades of dormancy, the Sierra Foothills wine industry began its modern revival in the 1970s. Pioneering winemakers recognized the region's potential:

- High-elevation vineyards (1,500–3,000 feet)
- Warm days and cool nights
- Well-drained granitic soils
- Surviving old-vine Zinfandel plantings

**1987:** The Sierra Foothills AVA was officially established (November 18), becoming the nation's 96th and California's 52nd appellation.

## Today

The region now boasts:

- **200+ wineries** — mostly small, family-owned operations
- **5,700 acres** under vine
- **5 sub-AVAs:** California Shenandoah Valley, El Dorado, Fair Play, Fiddletown, North Yuba
- **48 grape varietals** cultivated

The Mother Lode has returned to its roots as a significant California wine region, known for:

- **Old-vine Zinfandel** — the flagship grape
- **Rhône varietals** — Syrah, Viognier, Grenache
- **Italian varietals** — Barbera, Sangiovese
- **Spanish varietals** — Tempranillo, Albariño
- **High-altitude viticulture** — cooler temps, longer hang time
