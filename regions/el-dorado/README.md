# El Dorado County

> The northern anchor of the Mother Lode wine region

## Overview

El Dorado County sits at the north end of California's famed Mother Lode — the 120-mile gold vein discovered in the late 1840s that sparked the Gold Rush. Today, the region is better known for its high-altitude vineyards, old-vine Zinfandel, and diverse grape varietals.

## AVAs

### El Dorado AVA
- **Established:** 1983
- **Area:** El Dorado County (subset)
- **Characteristics:** Mountain vineyards, wide elevation range, granitic soils

### Fair Play AVA
- **Established:** 1996
- **Area:** High-elevation plateau east of Plymouth
- **Elevation:** 2,000–3,000 ft
- **Characteristics:** Volcanic soils, extreme diurnal temperature swings, known for Rhône varietals

## Climate

- **Days:** Warm to hot (Region II-III)
- **Nights:** Cool due to elevation
- **Diurnal swing:** 40–50°F common
- **Growing season:** 200–270 days
- **Rainfall:** 25–35 inches annually

## Signature Varietals

- **Zinfandel** — Many old-vine plantings
- **Syrah** — Thrives at elevation
- **Cabernet Sauvignon**
- **Barbera** — Italian heritage
- **Viognier** — Aromatic whites
- **Tempranillo** — Spanish varietals gaining ground

## History

El Dorado County was among the most planted wine regions in 19th-century California. At its peak (c. 1890), the county had approximately 8,000 acres under vine — 80% of the entire foothills' vineyard acreage. The industry collapsed due to phylloxera, population decline, and Prohibition.

The modern revival began in the 1970s. Greg and Sue Boeger established Boeger Winery in 1972, becoming the first bonded winery in El Dorado County since Prohibition.
