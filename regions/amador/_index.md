# Amador County

> Heart of the Shenandoah Valley — home to California's oldest Zinfandel vines

## Overview

Amador County lies at the heart of the Mother Lode wine region and is home to some of California's oldest continuously-producing Zinfandel vineyards. The county's winemaking heritage dates to the Gold Rush, and many vineyards trace their rootstock to 19th-century plantings.

## AVAs

### California Shenandoah Valley AVA
- **Established:** 1983
- **Area:** Amador & El Dorado counties
- **Elevation:** 1,200–2,000 ft
- **Characteristics:** Sandy clay loam, decomposed granite, sheltered valley
- **Note:** Named by settlers from Virginia in tribute to the Eastern Shenandoah

### Fiddletown AVA
- **Established:** 1983
- **Area:** East of Plymouth
- **Elevation:** 1,500–2,500 ft
- **Characteristics:** Higher, cooler than Shenandoah; decomposed granite soils
- **Known for:** Age-worthy Zinfandel, intense concentration

## Climate

- **Days:** Warm, dry summers
- **Nights:** Cool due to elevation
- **Diurnal swing:** 35–45°F
- **Growing season:** 250+ days
- **Rainfall:** 25–30 inches annually

## Historic Vineyards

Amador County is home to several of California's oldest Zinfandel vineyards:

- **Grandpère Vineyard** — Planted 1869
- **Deaver Vineyard** — Old-vine blocks from 1880s
- **Eschen Vineyard** — Pre-Prohibition heritage

## History

The California Shenandoah Valley was among the first wine regions established during the Gold Rush. The D'Agostini Winery (now Sobon Estate) was founded in 1856 — the fourth oldest winery in California.

By 1890, over 100 wineries operated across the Mother Lode, with Amador County as a major hub. Unlike many regions, some Amador vineyards survived Prohibition by selling grapes for home winemaking (legal at the time).
